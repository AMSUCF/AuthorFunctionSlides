How to Write at the End of the World (title)

Anastasia Salter
Professor of English, UCF
President, Electronic Literature Organization

--
Abstract (not visible, use for reference) This talk addresses a question I am frequently asked: Why do I keep writing books? The implied follow-up—does anyone read academic monographs?—points to a broader cultural anxiety. The forms of writing this celebration honors are all, we are told, suffering from a decline in audience. Headlines proclaim the death of literacy and the steady erosion of reading for pleasure. This alleged death of reading, exaggerated though it may be, arrives at a moment when the capacity of computational tools to read and write is expanding rapidly. Large language models are marketed as bringing "PhD-level intelligence" to their tasks. How do we reckon with authorship in the age of AI and amid conditions of widespread anti-intellectualism? What, indeed, is the purpose of our books?

in my field (electronic literature) we've been talking about the end of books for a long time - the hypertext novel was supposed to be the beginning of the end (cite 1992, Coover on end of books - image: coover)

but what happened instead was a different story: one shaped by the platforms on which we author and read on, and the question of who controls them

I'm going to take you through that journey today, but through the lens of my own authorship, with apologies for the navelgazing
